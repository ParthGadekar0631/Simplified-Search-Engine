Dogged by Trump, Chris Krebs Resigns From SentinelOneDogged by Trump, Chris Krebs Resigns From SentinelOne

The president revoked the former CISA director's security clearance, half a decade after Krebs challenged right-wing election disinformation, prompting his eventual resignation.

Nate Nelson, Contributing Writer

April 17, 2025

4 Min Read
Source: UPI via Alamy Stock Photo

A week after he and his employer were formally targeted by the Trump administration, Chris Krebs has resigned from his position at SentinelOne.

The yearslong drama between the president and the former director of the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) began, and might have seemed to have ended, in late 2020. But four and a half years later, on April 9 of this year, Donald J. Trump's White House published a presidential memorandum instructing the attorney general and secretary of homeland security to investigate Krebs' "activities" since he became CISA director in 2018. The document labeled him "a significant bad-faith actor who weaponized and abused his Government authority" to censor free speech and information about risks to elections, among other things.

In addition, the president revoked Krebs' security clearance, as well as those belonging to his colleagues at SentinelOne. According to a statement from SentinelOne, fewer than ten employees were affected.

But yesterday, in response, Krebs resigned his position as the company's chief intelligence and public policy officer. "I want to be clear: this is my decision, and mine alone," he emphasized in a letter to his colleagues, adding that "this is my fight, not the company's." He concluded the LinkedIn post in which he published the letter with the mock-Latin aphorism "Illegitimi non carborundum," which means "Don't let the bastards grind you down."

Trump's History With Krebs

Krebs and Trump first came into conflict in 2020 when, following Trump's election loss to Joe Biden, Trump began his infamous campaign to convince the public that the race was actually rigged against him.

As the head of the agency responsible for federal election security, Krebs became an outspoken voice against the disinformation spread by Trump and his cohort. CISA published a press release deeming the 2020 election "the most secure in American history." Krebs publicly called one of the Trump team's conspiracy theories about vote switching a "hoax," and tweeted that election manipulation rumors "have been unsubstantiated or are technically incoherent."

The same day he published that tweet, Nov. 17, 2020, Trump tweeted in response, "The recent statement by Chris Krebs on the security of the 2020 election was highly inaccurate. . . . Therefore, effective immediately, Chris Krebs has been terminated as Director of the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency."

View post on X

Later that month, in a television interview with Newsmax, a right-wing political outlet, a lawyer for the president expressed his desire for further punishment that the former CISA director "should be drawn and quartered. Taken out at dawn and shot."

Implications for Other Security Pros

Some in the cybersecurity community worry that by carrying out this vendetta against a political rival, the president is demeaning his office, setting a dangerous precedent, and potentially threatening national cybersecurity.

"The revocation of security clearances is more theater than meaningful," says John Bambenek, president at Bambenek Consulting. "It's possible to have discussion on the role of government in countering disinformation without the petty payback, which is only resulting in fewer people thinking about government service at a time where the government needs substantially more talent."

Lumifi Cyber field chief information security officer (CISO) Mike Hamilton also laments that "the implications of the revocation of security clearances make it increasingly difficult for those in similar positions to communicate objectively. The community overall is looking at this in the same way as other retaliatory actions were seeing, and those in positions with national significance are being much more careful in how messages are packaged."

Not everyone agrees on where the blame lies in this story, though. Philip Lieberman, CEO of Analog Informatics, believes, "This is an example of a security professional getting involved in politics and paying the price of picking sides, rather than staying on mission. It's a message to the rest of the intelligence community to stay out of politics."

Krebs, for his part, appears to understand that his decision to step in on the truth about the 2020 election would never be consequence-free. "As I said in the email [to SentinelOne colleagues], 'For those who know me, you know I dont shy away from tough fights,'" he wrote in the LinkedIn post. "'But I also know this is one I need to take on fully outside of SentinelOne.'"
